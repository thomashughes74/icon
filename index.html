Youth Sports Participation Rates Plummet During Pandemic

The pandemic caused a decline in hours spent playing sports among youth

By: Thomas Hughes

October 14, 2021

A basketball hoop in New York City has its rim removed during the summer of 2020 to prevent play. Credit: Thomas Hughes

When the pandemic hit, US sports were heavily affected. Participation rates declined sharply, especially in team sports. Bicycling, which was the most popular sporting activity among kids ages 6-17 before the pandemic hit, largely kept its participants active. But other sports, like basketball, baseball, and soccer, saw participation rates of active players go down by about half. This means that players who normally spent an hour playing their given sport played only a half hour during the pandemic.

The 2020 survey was conducted by the Aspen Institute in conjunction with Utah State University. The benefits of sports on mental helath, especially among youth, have been documented in studies including a 2019 study published in the academic journal, "journal of sports." Its author, Chandrakanta Hiremath, writes, "youth who actively involved themselves in sports both middle & high school showed more positive attitude towards life when compared to non-sport participants which means that less of suicidal ideation among sports participants (Taliaferro et al., 2011)."

Sources: Aspen Institute, Utah State University, Journal of Sports, The Noun Project: Maxim Kulikov/Econceptive/zidney/Mada Creative. Data: https://www.aspenprojectplay.org/state-of-play-2020/ages-13-17, https://www.aspenprojectplay.org/state-of-play-2020/ages-6-12, https://www.aspenprojectplay.org/state-of-play-2020/pandemic-trends

1. Bicycling

Before the pandemic hit, biking had the highest participation rates among youth sports, of 9.2 million kids. While not always organized, biking was the activity that most children ages 6-17 engaged in on a weekly basis. Biking rates declined the least during the pandemic, with bicyclists riding for 9.1 hours during the week, compared to 10.5 before.

2. Basketball

With about 7.5 million participants, basketball was the most popular team sport among youth participants in 2019. Many gyms were closed during the pandemic and some programs cancelled. In New York City, rims were even taken off of public courts early in the pandemic to discourage gathering and playing. By Sept. 2020, participants reported playing about 7.9 hours per week, compared to 14 hours before the nationwide shutdowns.

3. Baseball

Baseball also saw a decline of almost half as much time spent playing in 2020. With 6.4 million players before the pandemic, those who resumed during 2020 played about 7.6 hours per week, down from 14 hours in 2019.

4. Soccer

Soccer ranked fourth in overall youth participation, with 3.7 million minors playing the sport. Despite the ability to play outdoors, the sport saw a decline from 11.1 hours per week for participants, to about 6.2 hours during the pandemic.
